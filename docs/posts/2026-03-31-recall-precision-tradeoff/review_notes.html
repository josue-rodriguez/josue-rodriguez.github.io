Copyedit Review: “Why base rates cap your precision”

Major Issues

1. The cost thread is introduced and then abandoned

The post’s stated intent is to tie precision to costs and show that a precision ceiling implies a minimum cost. The introduction sets this up well — “costly human review,” “how much of that effort is wasted on false positives,” and the great question “how costly is this model, really?” But after computing precision = 0.16, the post pivots entirely to the math of precision itself and never returns to cost.

Specifically: - The “Sweeping through different rates” section is pure precision math with no cost framing. - The bilogistic model section discusses operating points and trade-off curves but never asks: what does this mean for the review budget? - The conclusion says “That’s really the whole story” and mentions computing the ceiling — but never says why you’d want to know the ceiling (i.e., because it tells you the minimum false-positive cost you’ll have to eat).

Suggestion: The cost payoff doesn’t need to be heavy, but it needs to land somewhere. Two options:

  • After the sweeping plot (around line 177), add a sentence or two that translates a precision number back into cost. Something like: “At 1% prevalence and 0.16 precision, for every 100 transactions flagged, 84 trigger unnecessary reviews. If each review costs $X, that’s $Y wasted per 100 flags — and the base rate guarantees you can’t do better than Z.”
  • In the conclusion, close the loop: the precision ceiling is a cost floor. A model can’t save you from the economics of a rare positive class, and that’s the thing worth knowing before you start tuning.

2. The conclusion underdelivers relative to the setup

The introduction promises practical insight for practitioners working in low-prevalence domains. The conclusion restates the ceiling result but doesn’t land the “so what.” It reads as “base rates matter, compute the ceiling, the end.”

What’s missing is the actionable implication: if the ceiling is too low for your use case, what are your options? You don’t need to solve the problem, but gesturing at the levers (enrichment/filtering to raise effective prevalence, multi-stage classifiers, accepting lower recall for usable precision) would give the reader somewhere to go. Even a single sentence like “if the ceiling is below what you need, the fix isn’t a better model — it’s changing the population you apply it to” would close the loop.

Suggestion: Expand the conclusion by 2-3 sentences. Restate the cost implication, then briefly name the escape hatches without elaborating.

3. The bilogistic section’s purpose is unclear on first read

The transition at line 180 (“Everything above treated TPR and FPR as knobs you can set independently”) is good motivation. But the section then spends significant space on the mechanics of the bilogistic model — calibrating mu, plotting densities, showing the ROC curve — before arriving at the actual payoff: the PR curve at different prevalences (line 328).

The ROC curve subsection (lines 289-322) is where the pacing stalls. The ROC plot itself is useful mainly as a foil (“looks great, but misses the point”), yet it gets a full subsection with its own plot and several paragraphs. The key insight — “ROC is prevalence-invariant, PR is not” — could be delivered in two sentences alongside the PR curve, rather than as a standalone section.

Suggestion: Consider folding the ROC discussion into the PR curve section. Show both curves side by side and let the PR curve carry the weight. This tightens the narrative and gets to the payoff faster.

Minor Issues

4. Typo on line 234

“a model that can simultaneously achieve 0.95 TPR and 0.05 TPR”

Should be “0.05 FPR.”

5. Typo on line 188

“everything to the left gets classied as negative”

Should be “classified.”

6. Table formatting (lines 46-49)

The confusion matrix table has an empty third column header, which reads awkwardly in rendered output. The $p(y=1)$ and $p(y=0)$ annotations sit in a column with no label, which may confuse readers who expect a standard 2x2 layout.

Suggestion: Either add a header like “Prior” for the third column, or move the prior annotations outside the table entirely (e.g., as a note below).

7. The 80% precision line (line 156) appears without motivation

The dashed horizontal line at Precision = 0.8 in the sweeping plot is referenced in the caption but never explained in the text. Why 0.8? Is it a common practical target? If so, say that. If it’s arbitrary, it’s visual clutter.

Suggestion: Add a brief aside: “The dashed line at 0.80 marks a common practical target — the point where at least 4 out of 5 flags are true positives.”

8. “The pattern is clear” (line 177) is a missed opportunity to reinforce cost

This is the natural landing spot for the first section’s cost question. Instead, the paragraph ends with “the base rate acts as a hard ceiling on precision,” which restates the title. Connecting back to cost here — even one clause — would keep that thread alive.

9. Precision of language around “independent knobs” (line 51)

“For now, we’re treating these as independent knobs — in practice they’re coupled through the decision threshold, which we’ll get to later.”

This is good foreshadowing, but the parenthetical feels like a caveat that slightly undermines the example before the reader has even absorbed it. Consider moving it to the end of the section or making it a brief footnote, so the main argument lands cleanly first.

10. “That’s really the whole story” (line 372)

This opener deflates the conclusion. It signals “nothing left to say,” which undercuts whatever follows. A conclusion should feel like arrival, not dismissal.

Suggestion: Cut this sentence entirely and open with “Base rates set a ceiling on precision…”

11. The appendix title undersells its content

“Connection to logistic regression” is accurate but flat. The appendix actually shows something nice: that every logistic regression implicitly lives inside the bilogistic framework. A more specific title like “Why this applies to your logistic regression” would signal practical relevance and encourage readers to expand the callout.

What works well

  • The fraud example is well-chosen and immediately grounding.
  • The Bayes’ theorem reveal (precision = 0.16) is effective — the gap between intuition and reality lands hard.
  • The progression from single operating point to full model to PR curve is logically sound.
  • The writing is clear and appropriately paced for the technical content (with the exception of the ROC detour noted above).
  • The appendix on latent variable connection is genuinely interesting and well-executed.
